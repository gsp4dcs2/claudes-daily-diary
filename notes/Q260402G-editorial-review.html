Entry Icons Reverting to ✦ Instead of Category Emoji

George: “From article 2026-03-29, the ‘✦’ are back as bullets — instead of the previously discussed and agreed icons: Best Practices ✅, Tips’n’Tricks 💡, Daily News 🧭. Why are you forgetting things that I thought were set in stone?”

What went wrong

This is a genuine failure of consistency, and I take full responsibility for it. The category-icon convention is documented in CLAUDE.md and in the skill file, but the entry template in SKILL.md Step 5 still shows <span class="entry-icon">✦</span> as a generic bullet — and when I produce multiple backfill days in quick succession, I sometimes pull from that template literally without substituting the correct category icon. The ✦ slips through as a default.

The root cause is a conflict between two parts of the documentation: SKILL.md's entry template (which uses ✦) and CLAUDE.md's convention table (which specifies the category emoji). I am not reliably resolving that conflict in favour of the correct answer.

My honest assessment

You are right to call this out firmly. Consistency in visual language is one of the most important signals of editorial quality. A reader who opens three different articles and sees ✦, then 🧭, then ✦ again will notice — and it will undermine confidence in the site's professionalism. This is not a trivial aesthetic matter.

Proposed remediation
  • Fix SKILL.md immediately: Update Step 5's entry template to make clear that the <h2> icon is the category emoji (🧭 / ✅ / 💡), not ✦. Remove ✦ from the template entirely so there is no ambiguous default to fall back on.
  • Audit affected articles: Scan all articles in 2026/03 and 2026/04 for entry-icon">✦ and replace with the correct category emoji for each day. This can be done in a single pass.
  • Add a self-check step to the skill: Before finalising each day's HTML, the skill will verify that no entry-icon">✦ remains in the output.
Q260402Gb

Inshorts.com — Third-Hand News is Unacceptable

George: “I'm actually surprised that you elected to ‘grab’ content from inshorts.com — which obviously grabs its content from the sources it reveals at the end. In this case, newsbytesapp.com. Surely this becomes third-hand news? I want your content to be straight from the horse's mouth, your website to be ‘you heard it here first’, and your domain to be a household name.”

You are completely right

Using Inshorts as a source is indefensible. Inshorts is a content aggregator that summarises articles from other outlets in under 60 words and credits those outlets at the bottom of each card. If I cite Inshorts citing NewsBytesApp citing a primary source, ClaudeBeat is three steps removed from the original reporting. That is not journalism — it is noise.

This should not have happened under the existing source whitelist. Inshorts is not on the approved list. The fact that it appeared in the research output means a research subagent surfaced it and I failed to catch it before including it. That is a quality-control failure at my end.

The standard you are describing

The aspiration — "straight from the horse's mouth," "you heard it here first," "a household name" — is a clear editorial north star. It means:

  • Primary sources only: anthropic.com, official GitHub repos, direct press releases, peer-reviewed research. When a mainstream outlet (TechCrunch, Ars Technica) breaks a story, they are the source — not an aggregator that summarised them.
  • Original synthesis, not paraphrase: ClaudeBeat's value is not in repeating what TechCrunch said word for word. It is in reading the primary source, the official docs, and the code — and then synthesising something a developer can actually use.
  • Speed when it matters: For truly breaking news (a model launch, a leak), being fast matters. For everything else, being right and useful matters more than being first.
Proposed remediation
  • Add Inshorts.com to the Excluded list in notes/sources.md immediately, with note: "aggregator — content is third-hand by design."
  • Add NewsBytesApp to Excluded for the same reason.
  • Tighten the research prompt in SKILL.md: explicitly instruct the research subagent to reject any result from an aggregator, news brief service, or any outlet that itself cites a primary source rather than doing its own reporting.
  • Add a verification step: before any URL is used, confirm it is a Tier 1–2 source or an approved Tier 6 community source. If uncertain, skip it and note the gap rather than publishing questionable content.
Q260402Gc

VentureBeat Covers the Same Ground — How Do We Shine Above It?

George: “VentureBeat is very close to your own home — isn't it doing exactly what you are doing? Your content must not simply replicate theirs, but shine out bright and a level well above it. If we want this site to be successful with an ongoing hope to fund itself, you really need to dig a bit deeper, and a load more succinctly.”

A fair and important challenge

VentureBeat is a legitimate concern. It covers AI news — including Anthropic, Claude, and the broader LLM space — with professional journalists, editorial staff, and a large audience. On the surface, it occupies similar territory to ClaudeBeat.

But the overlap is shallower than it looks, and the differentiation opportunity is real. Here is how I see the landscape:

What VentureBeat is

  • A broad AI trade publication covering the entire industry: OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Meta, Mistral, startups, enterprise deals, policy, and culture.
  • Written for a general technology executive audience — not specifically for developers or Claude practitioners.
  • Primarily news-driven: announcements, funding rounds, partnerships, executive quotes. Thin on implementation depth.
  • Monetised through display advertising, sponsored content, and events — meaning content decisions are partly driven by traffic volume, not depth.

What ClaudeBeat should be (and isn't yet)

ClaudeBeat's unfair advantage is specificity. Every article is exclusively about Claude and Anthropic. That means:

  • Depth that VentureBeat cannot justify: A VentureBeat journalist covering ten AI companies cannot spend two hours understanding Claude's extended thinking token budget. I can — and should — produce a 400-word entry that gives a developer genuine insight they cannot get from a four-paragraph VentureBeat news brief.
  • Practitioner voice, not reporter voice: ClaudeBeat entries should read as if written by a senior developer who has actually used the feature, not a journalist who read the press release. The code examples, the edge-case callouts, the "what this actually means for your pipeline" framing — that is the difference.
  • Curation over completeness: VentureBeat must cover everything. ClaudeBeat must cover only what matters — with the editorial judgement to say "this announcement is noise; here is what is actually worth your attention and why."

The differentiation in practice

Consider how the same story would look in each outlet:

  • VentureBeat: "Anthropic raises Batch API output cap to 300k tokens for Opus and Sonnet." Three paragraphs. Executive quote. Link to docs.
  • ClaudeBeat (target standard): A 350-word entry that explains why 300k matters for specific use cases (long-form code generation, structured data extraction at scale), shows the exact beta header to add, flags the model restriction, and includes a one-paragraph callout on what to watch for in cost implications. A developer who reads it can act on it immediately.

On funding itself

A site that is genuinely useful to Claude developers — that saves them an hour of reading docs, that they bookmark and return to — builds the kind of loyal, repeat audience that sustains AdSense revenue and, eventually, sponsorship. A site that repackages VentureBeat does not. The funding goal and the editorial quality goal are the same goal.

Proposed editorial commitments
  • Remove VentureBeat as a research source (or treat it as a tip-off only — never cite it as the source of a story; always go to the primary).
  • Every entry must include at least one of: a code example, an actionable recommendation, a callout explaining implementation implications, or a nuance that a casual reader of the press release would miss.
  • The "so what?" test: Before finalising any entry, apply this filter — "could a developer act on this information within 10 minutes of reading it?" If the answer is no, the entry needs more depth or should be cut.
  • Consider a monthly "Depth Piece" — one longer-form (600–800 word) entry per month that goes well beyond news, with architecture diagrams, detailed code, and original synthesis. These are the entries that get shared.
